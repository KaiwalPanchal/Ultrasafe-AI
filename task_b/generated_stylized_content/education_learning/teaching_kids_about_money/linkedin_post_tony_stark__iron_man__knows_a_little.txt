Money talks — but kids? They barely speak the language. Ready to flip that script?

Teaching kids about money isn’t just handing them coins or allowances and hoping they figure it out. It’s about equipping the next generation with the tools to make smart, confident money moves — before they even know to ask “What’s a credit score?” According to the Council for Economic Education, only 22 states require personal finance courses in high school. Meanwhile, 43% of adults can’t answer basic money questions. Guess what? Starting early boosts those odds big time.

Here’s the deal: kids need to get that money isn’t magic — it’s a medium of exchange. They should know you earn it (hello, chores!), save it to hit goals, budget like a boss, and spend wisely (needs vs. wants, anyone?). Toss in a dash of generosity, and you’ve got a financial foundation that sticks.

Age 3 to 5? Turn your living room into a mini-market — role play is their money lab. Ages 6 to 8? Introduce the triple-jar system — Spend, Save, Give — and watch those budgeting muscles flex. Tweens and teens? Bring in the banking basics and credit talk, with hands-on practice. 

Forget the myth that allowances alone build money smarts — guidance is the secret sauce. And no, chatting cash won’t stress the kids out. The data says the opposite: open talks build confidence.

Experts like Dr. Annamaria Lusardi highlight that teaching compound interest early isn’t rocket science — it’s game-changing. And with apps like Greenlight and BusyKid transforming allowances into digital experiences, kids are learning money management in style.

Parents, educators, policymakers — this isn’t just a nice-to-have. It’s a must. Model the behavior. Integrate money lessons everywhere. Fund those programs. Because if you want smart money moves tomorrow, you gotta start today.
