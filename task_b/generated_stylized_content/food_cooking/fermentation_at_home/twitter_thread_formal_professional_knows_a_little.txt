1/10  
Fermentation at home is an ancient technique that transforms sugars using microorganisms like bacteria and yeast under controlled conditions. It preserves food, enhances flavors, and improves nutritional value. Popular examples include kombucha, kimchi, and yogurt.

2/10  
Kombucha is a fermented tea using a symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast (SCOBY). The process lasts 7-14 days at 20-30°C, producing acids and trace ethanol. Studies show commercial kombucha contains 10⁶ to 10⁸ CFU/mL of probiotics.

3/10  
Kimchi is a traditional Korean dish of fermented vegetables such as napa cabbage and radish. It ferments 1-4 weeks at 0-10°C, relying on lactic acid bacteria like *Lactobacillus kimchii*, reaching microbial counts of 10⁷ to 10⁹ CFU/g.

4/10  
Yogurt results from fermenting milk with bacteria like *Lactobacillus delbrueckii* subsp. *bulgaricus* and *Streptococcus thermophilus*. Incubation occurs 4-8 hours at 40-45°C, yielding 10⁷ to 10⁹ CFU/g probiotics in commercial products.

5/10  
It is essential to note fermentation produces probiotics and vitamins, such as B-complex, which may support gut health. Successful fermentation depends on selecting the correct substrate, microbial cultures, temperature, oxygen levels, and sanitation.

6/10  
To brew kombucha at home, dissolve sugar in tea, add a SCOBY and starter liquid, then ferment 7-14 days in a covered jar with breathable cloth. Kimchi preparation involves salting vegetables, mixing with spice paste, and fermenting in a cool environment for several weeks.

7/10  
For yogurt, pasteurize milk, cool it to approximately 43°C, then inoculate with live cultures. Incubate in a warm place until coagulation, usually within hours. Straining or flavoring can be done post-fermentation as desired.

8/10  
Several misconceptions exist: fermentation is safe when proper hygiene and cultures are used, refrigeration during fermentation is not always required, and not all probiotics survive after fermentation. Additionally, fermented foods vary widely in alcohol content and flavor.

9/10  
Experts recommend using reliable starter cultures, maintaining sanitation, and controlling temperature to ensure safety and reproducibility. Secondary fermentation in kombucha can enhance carbonation, while monitoring pH (3.0–4.5) confirms fermentation progress.

10/10  
Considering these factors, home fermentation of kombucha, kimchi, and yogurt offers a scalable, health-promoting practice.
