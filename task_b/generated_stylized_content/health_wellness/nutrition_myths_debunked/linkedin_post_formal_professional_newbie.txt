I am pleased to share an important overview addressing common nutrition myths. For those new to nutrition, understanding the basics is essential to making healthy choices.

Nutrition involves the food components our bodies need to function well. A balanced diet includes a variety of foods to provide carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, and minerals. No single food can meet all our nutritional needs. Calories from these nutrients provide energy, and managing the balance of intake and use helps maintain a healthy weight.

Here are key points to remember: 

• Fiber supports digestion and heart health, yet many people do not consume the recommended 25–30 grams daily.  
• Added sugars should be limited to under 6 teaspoons (25 grams) for women and 9 teaspoons (36 grams) for men to reduce risks like diabetes and heart disease.  
• Nutrient deficiencies, such as iron or vitamin A shortage, affect billions worldwide, highlighting the need for varied diets.  
• Healthy fats from sources like nuts and olive oil are important, while avoiding harmful trans fats.  
• Skipping meals or relying on supplements instead of whole foods may harm long-term health.  
• Detox diets have no scientific backing; the body naturally removes toxins through liver and kidneys.

Practical steps include using guidelines like the USDA MyPlate to plan balanced meals, reading nutrition labels carefully, and consulting registered dietitians or healthcare professionals for advice tailored to your needs.

Current trends show growing interest in plant-based diets, personalized nutrition based on genetics, and reducing ultra-processed foods. These support both individual health and sustainability goals.

Starting with a solid foundation in nutrition helps build lifelong healthy habits. Please feel free to reach out with any questions or to share your experiences. Together, let’s explore the basics of good nutrition.
