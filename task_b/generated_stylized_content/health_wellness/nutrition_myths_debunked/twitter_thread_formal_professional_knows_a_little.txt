1/11  
Nutrition is vital to health and wellbeing. However, myths and misinformation frequently obscure facts, impacting dietary choices. This thread clarifies common nutrition myths with evidence-based explanations to support informed decisions. #NutritionFacts #Health

2/11  
A balanced diet is crucial. The WHO defines it as diverse foods providing adequate macronutrients (carbohydrates, proteins, fats) and micronutrients (vitamins, minerals). No single food can fulfill all bodily nutrient requirements.

3/11  
Calories determine weight management. The CDC notes carbs and proteins offer 4 calories per gram; fats provide 9 calories per gram. Balancing calorie intake with expenditure is essential for maintaining a healthy weight.

4/11  
Micronutrient deficiencies remain widespread. According to the Global Burden of Disease Study, iron, vitamin A, and iodine deficiencies affect billions, emphasizing the need for dietary diversity to prevent these conditions.

5/11  
Dietary fiber promotes digestive health, blood sugar regulation, and cholesterol reduction, per the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics. Most individuals consume about 17 grams daily, below the recommended 25â€“30 grams.

6/11  
Limiting added sugars is important. The American Heart Association advises women to consume less than 6 teaspoons (25 grams) and men less than 9 teaspoons (36 grams) daily. Excess sugars correlate with obesity, diabetes, and heart disease.

7/11  
Several myths persist: Carbohydrates are not inherently harmful; they are vital energy sources, especially complex carbs with fiber. Dietary fats are essential for hormones and vitamin absorption; type matters. Skipping meals disrupts metabolism and may cause overeating.

8/11  
Detox diets lack scientific support; the body naturally detoxifies via the liver and kidneys. Supplements can address specific deficiencies but cannot replace the comprehensive benefits of whole foods.

9/11  
Experts recommend focusing on whole foods and balanced diets rather than excluding entire food groups. Personalized nutrition plans tailored to individual needs promote sustainable health outcomes.

10/11  
Current trends include plant-based diets for health and sustainability, personalized nutrition using genetics and microbiome insights, and reducing ultra-processed foods linked to chronic diseases. Sustainable diets consider both nutrition and environmental impact.

11/11  
A key takeaway is to adopt balanced meals, limit sugars and processed foods, include healthy fats, and stay hydrated. Consult professionals for personalized advice and critically evaluate diet claims. Reliable sources like WHO and CDC offer trustworthy information. #HealthyEating #NutritionScience
